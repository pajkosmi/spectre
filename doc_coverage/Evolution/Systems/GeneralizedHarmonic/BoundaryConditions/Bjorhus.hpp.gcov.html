      52             :  * \brief Sets constraint preserving boundary conditions using the Bjorhus

      54             :  *
      55             :  * \details Boundary conditions for the generalized harmonic evolution system
      56             :  * can be divided in to three parts, constraint-preserving, physical and gauge
      57             :  * boundary conditions.
      58             :  *
      59             :  * The generalized harmonic (GH) evolution system is a first-order reduction of
      60             :  * Einstein equations brought about by the imposition of GH gauge. This
      61             :  * introduces constraints on the free (evolved) variables in addition to the
      62             :  * standard Hamiltonian and momentum constraints. The constraint-preserving
      63             :  * portion of the boundary conditions is designed to prevent the influx of
      64             :  * constraint violations from external faces of the evolution domain, by damping
      65             :  * them away on a controlled and short time-scale. These conditions are imposed
      66             :  * as corrections to the characteristic projections of the right-hand-sides of
      67             :  * the GH evolution equations (i.e. using Bjorhus' method \cite Bjorhus1995),
      68             :  * as written down in Eq. (63) - (65) of \cite Lindblom2005qh . In addition to
      69             :  * these equations, the fourth projection is simply frozen in the unlikely case
      70             :  * its coordinate speed becomes negative, i.e. \f$d_t u^{\hat{1}+}{}_{ab}=0\f$
      71             :  * (in the notation of \cite Lindblom2005qh). The gauge degrees
      72             :  * of freedom are controlled by imposing a Sommerfeld-type condition (\f$L=0\f$
      73             :  * member of the hierarchy derived in \cite BaylissTurkel) that allow gauge
      74             :  * perturbations to pass through the boundary without strong reflections. These
      75             :  * assume a spherical outer boundary, and can be written down as in Eq. (25) of
      76             :  * \cite Rinne2007ui . Finally, the physical boundary conditions control the
      77             :  * influx of inward propagating gravitational-wave solutions from the external
      78             :  * boundaries. These are derived by considering the evolution system of the Weyl
      79             :  * curvature tensor, and controlling the inward propagating characteristics of
      80             :  * the system that are proportional to the Newman-Penrose curvature spinor
      81             :  * components \f$\Psi_4\f$ and \f$\Psi_0\f$. Here we use Eq. (68) of
      82             :  * \cite Lindblom2005qh to disallow any incoming waves. It is to be noted that
      83             :  * all the above conditions are also imposed on characteristic modes with speeds
      84             :  * exactly zero.
      85             :  *
      86             :  * This class provides two choices of combinations of the above corrections:
      87             :  *  - `ConstraintPreserving` : this imposes the constraint-preserving and
      88             :  * gauge-controlling corrections;
      89             :  *  - `ConstraintPreservingPhysical` : this additionally restricts the influx of
      90             :  * any physical gravitational waves from the outer boundary, in addition to
      91             :  * preventing the influx of constraint violations and gauge perturbations.
      92             :  *
      93             :  * We refer to `Bjorhus::constraint_preserving_bjorhus_corrections_dt_v_psi()`,
      94             :  * `Bjorhus::constraint_preserving_bjorhus_corrections_dt_v_zero()`,
      95             :  * `Bjorhus::constraint_preserving_bjorhus_corrections_dt_v_minus()`, and
      96             :  * `Bjorhus::constraint_preserving_physical_bjorhus_corrections_dt_v_minus()`
      97             :  * for the further details on implementation.
      98             :  *
      99             :  * \note These boundary conditions assume a spherical outer boundary. Also, we
     100             :  * do not yet have an option to inject incoming gravitational waves at the outer
     101             :  * boundary.
     102             :  */
